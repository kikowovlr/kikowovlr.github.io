Can Humans Really Move?

1. Technological Feasibility üß≠

We're not ready yet. No spacecraft exists today that can send large numbers of humans to another planet, let alone sustain them long-term.

Mars is the closest serious candidate, but even that would require multi-year missions, immense life support systems, and constant resupply or in-situ resource use (ISRU).

Interstellar travel (e.g., to Proxima b) would take tens of thousands of years with current propulsion.

2. Biological & Psychological Challenges üß¨

Radiation Exposure: Without Earth's magnetic field, space travelers face high cancer and illness risks.

Muscle and bone loss in low gravity (e.g., Mars = 0.38g) may cause long-term health issues.

Mental health risks from isolation, confinement, and time dilation (for far-off missions).

3. Logistical & Economic Realities üí∞

Astronomical cost: Even one Mars mission is expected to cost billions. Colonization? Likely trillions.

Infrastructure gap: We'd need habitats, power, food production, waste systems, and more ‚Äî built in a hostile environment with zero backup.

4. Should We Move? (Ethical + Environmental Angle) üåç

Some argue we should fix Earth before fleeing it.

Others say expanding beyond Earth ensures species survival in case of global catastrophe.

Terraforming also raises ethical concerns ‚Äî e.g., if microbial life already exists on a planet like Europa or Mars, do we have the right to change it?
